     8	    beta test, v.
     9	         To voluntarily entrust one's data, one's livelihood and one's
    10	         sanity to hardware or software intended to destroy all
    11	         three.  In earlier days, virgins were often selected to beta
    12	         test volcanos.
    13	    
    14	    bit, n.
    15	         A unit of measure applied to color.  Twenty-four-bit color
    16	         refers to expensive $3 color as opposed to the cheaper 25
    17	         cent, or two-bit, color that use to be available a few years
    18	         ago.
    19	    
    20	    buzzword, n.
    21	         The fly in the ointment of computer literacy.
    22	    
    23	    clone, n.
    24	         1. An exact duplicate, as in "our product is a clone of their
    25	         product." 2. A shoddy, spurious copy, as in "their product is
    26	         a clone of our product."
    27	    
    28	    enhance, v.
    29	         To tamper with an image, usually to its detriment.
    30	    
    31	    genlock, n.
    32	         Why he stays in the bottle.
    33	    
    34	    guru, n.
    35	         A computer owner who can read the manual.
    36	    
    37	    handshaking protocol, n.
    38	         A process employed by hostile hardware devices to initate a
    39	         terse but civil dialogue, which, in turn, is characterized by
    40	         occasional misunderstanding, sulking, and name-calling.
    41	    
    42	    italic, adj.
    43	         Slanted to the right to emphasize key phrases. Unique to
    44	         Western alphabets; in Eastern languages, the same phrases are
    45	         often slanted to the left.
    46	    
    47	    Japan, n.
    48	         A fictional place where elves, gnomes and economic
    49	         imperialists create electronic equipment and computers using
    50	         black magic.  It is said that in the capital city of
    51	         Akihabara, the streets are paved with gold and semiconductor
    52	         chips grow on low bushes from which they are harvested by the
    53	         happy natives.
    54	    
    55	    kern, v.
    56	         1. To pack type together as tightly as the kernels on an ear
    57	         of corn.  2. In parts of Brooklyn and Queens, N.Y., a small,
    58	         metal object used as part of the monetary system.
    59	    
    60	    modem, adj.
    61	         Up-to-date, new-fangled, as in "Thoroughly Modem Millie."  An
    62	         unfortunate byproduct of kerning.
    63	    
    64	    pixel, n.
    65	         A mischievous, magical spirit associated with screen
    66	         displays.  The computer industry has frequently borrowed from
    67	         mythology: Witness the sprites in computer graphics, the
    68	         demons in artificial intelligence, and the trolls in the
    69	         marketing department.
    70	    
    71	    prototype, n.
    72	         First stage in the life cycle of a computer product, folowed
    73	         by pre-alpha, alpha, beta, release version, corrected release
    74	         version, upgrade, corrected upgrade, etc.  Unlike its
    75	         successors, the prototype is not expected to work.
    76	    
    77	    revolutionary, adj.
    78	         Repackaged.
    79	    
    80	    Unix, N.
    81	         A computer operating system, once thought to be flabby and
    82	         impotent, that now shows a surprising interest in making off
    83	         with the workstation harem.
